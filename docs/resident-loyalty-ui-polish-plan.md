# Resident Loyalty UI Polish Plan

Date: 2026-06-14

## Purpose

This document sets the product-design bar for moving Living Rewards from MVP prototype toward a client-demo-ready launch surface. The goal is not to copy Bilt or any competitor. The goal is to understand what polished resident/rewards products signal visually, then apply those lessons to our operational-efficiency resident loyalty concept.

## References Reviewed

- Bilt app: https://www.bilt.com/app
- Bilt Alliance: https://alliance.biltrewards.com/
- Piñata: https://www.pinata.ai/

## Market Design Patterns

### Bilt App

What to learn:

- The product is app-first, not dashboard-first.
- The core mental model is simple: Rent, Neighborhood, Rewards.
- The app ties home and neighborhood together.
- Monthly reward moments create a reason to reopen the app.
- Redemption feels broad: rent, travel, everyday purchases, local merchants.
- Screenshots and phone imagery carry the pitch more than dense text.

What this means for Living Rewards:

- The resident wallet should be the primary demo artifact.
- The operator console should be proof, not the hero.
- The navigation should stay simple: Home, Earn, Nearby, Redeem, Activity.
- Rewards should feel like a marketplace, not a task list.

### Bilt Alliance

What to learn:

- The owner/operator pitch is framed as a hospitality platform for housing.
- The product connects resident, property, and neighborhood stacks.
- Renewals, move-in gifts, amenity bookings, neighborhood benefits, and resident services are positioned as one branded resident journey.

What this means for Living Rewards:

- The landlord value prop should be operational efficiency plus a better resident experience.
- Building brand can be centered without pretending to be a full PMS.
- Renewal interest, move-in checklists, access confirmation, and notice acknowledgement should be shown as resident-friendly hospitality moments.

### Piñata

What to learn:

- Piñata sells a simple renter outcome: rent can build credit and unlock rewards.
- It uses phone visuals, app-store CTAs, rewards-shop language, and clear consumer value.
- It makes benefits tangible with recognizable categories like gift cards, deals, and giveaways.

What this means for Living Rewards:

- Make every resident action answer, "What do I get?"
- Avoid internal terminology like "event ledger" in resident-facing UI.
- Use clear reward cards with value labels, unlock requirements, and status.

### Livly

What to learn:

- Livly positions around one branded resident operations experience.
- It combines resident mobile app, amenity booking, events, smart access, renewals, rewards, and property operations.
- The operator pitch is unified, simple, and enterprise credible.

What this means for Living Rewards:

- The manager console should look credible and operational, but not become the whole product.
- Show operational outcomes in executive language: fewer follow-ups, better records, faster scheduling, renewal visibility.
- Keep Level CRE's data patterns, but make the surface feel purpose-built.

### Amenify

What to learn:

- Amenify sells lifestyle services: cleaning, chores, food/grocery, handyman, dog walking, and home shopping.
- It uses service-marketplace categories, social proof, and resident convenience as the emotional hook.

What this means for Living Rewards:

- Nearby benefits should feel like real resident value, not placeholder cards.
- Lifestyle categories can broaden the product beyond rent and maintenance.
- Social proof and sample partner tiles would make the MVP feel more credible.

## Client-Ready UI Bar

A client-demo-ready MVP should meet this bar:

- The first five seconds communicate a new resident rewards product, not Level CRE.
- The resident app screen looks like the product, not an explanatory mock.
- The operator console looks intentionally secondary.
- There are real visual assets: property, neighborhood, resident wallet, offer tiles.
- The UI has consistent brand tokens: colors, radius, type scale, buttons, badges, spacing.
- Mobile is treated as primary for the resident view.
- Copy is resident-friendly and action-oriented.
- Demo-only limitations are visible but not dominant.
- The reward catalog feels tangible and varied.
- Operational value is quantified in plain terms.

## Recommended Next UI Pass

1. Create a tighter visual identity.
   - Refine logo mark, color tokens, surfaces, shadows, and type scale.
   - Reduce generic dark SaaS styling.

2. Build a more realistic resident app frame.
   - Add bottom navigation.
   - Add offer-detail, mission-complete, and reward-request states.
   - Add compact transaction/points history.

3. Upgrade the reward marketplace.
   - Add richer tiles for rent credit, dining, grocery, coffee, fitness, parking, fee waiver, and travel-style mock transfer.
   - Add locked/unlocked states.
   - Add "monthly drop" section with urgency.

4. Improve the operator console.
   - Use a clean B2B dashboard style.
   - Lead with four ROI metrics only.
   - Move raw ledger details into an expandable/admin-style section.

5. Add credibility signals.
   - Mock property brand.
   - Mock neighborhood partners.
   - Demo data freshness.
   - Clear "no payments/card rails/credit reporting yet" note.

6. Prepare for lunch-demo feedback.
   - Have three talking paths: resident value, landlord ops value, and implementation risk.
   - Ask whether the buyer cares most about maintenance photos, access confirmations, notices, renewals, or retention.

## Design Principle

Resident-facing surfaces should feel like rewards, convenience, and neighborhood value. Manager-facing surfaces should feel like less chasing, better records, and measurable operating lift.
